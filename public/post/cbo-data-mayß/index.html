CBO Data (May release)

Posted by Kyle Webb on Tuesday, September 10, 2019

The Congressional Budget Office’s May release of their data goes back to 1965 and records yearly debt estimates in US dollars (blue) and percentage of GDP (orange). According to the January release from the CBO, debt held by the public is “projected to grow steadily, reaching 93 percent of GDP in 2029 (its highest level since just after World War II) and about 150 percent of GDP in 2049—far higher than it has ever been.”

What I find interesting about this graph (besides the obvious upward trend in in the most recent years) is the drastic downward turn from 1993 to 2001. I looked into the details of the Clinton administration and more of the CBO data to get a clearer picture of this phenomenon.

Decreased defense spending?

Most people attribute the downward swing in percentage of GDP debt from 1993 to 2001 as the Clinton administration’s action to lower defense spending and a relatively low commitment to US military interests.

Outlays

An outlay is defined as a total cost or expenditure required or incurred in acquiring an asset, achieving an objective, or executing a decision. For the US government, this could mean a lot of things. One of the obvious markers for success in spending during the Clinton administration was the constant decrease in outlay spending relative to GDP. While most administrations were erratic in their outlay spending habits, the percentage of GDP for outlay spending decreased by approximately 0.4 % per year under the Clinton administration.

When diving deeper into the outlays, it becomes apparent why the years under the Clinton administration were reducing debt.

Revenue Increase

Revenue boomed during the 1990’s tech boom and the US government hauled in more from tax revenue than it ever had.
